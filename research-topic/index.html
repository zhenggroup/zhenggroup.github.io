Electrochemistry represents a significant aspect of modern physical chemistry, especially related to current energy topics. It is the art of playing the potential to control electrons to break and form bonding.
Our group adopts the research logic of: Understand, Design, Integration, including following topics.

in situ analysis of electrochemical interface

Electrochemical interface is where the electron transfer between electrode/electrocatalyst and electrolyte happens. Understanding the interface reveals the structure-property relationship of electrocatalysts. In situ techniques are useful to study the interface.
Our lab focuses on the following techniques: in situ UV-Vis and Raman spectroscopy, Atomic Force Microscopy, and X-ray diffraction.

Rational material design for electrocatalysis

Electrocatalysts are materials that can promote electrochemical conversion at fix potential. For best efficiency, the materials need to have suitable surface atomic configuration and adorption energy. The best way of achieving this is rational design of active sites.
Our lab focuses on the following reactions: carbon dioxide electrochemical adsorption and reduction; electrochemical organic synthesis; water splitting.

Integrated electrocatalytic system

Electrochemical conversion is not only about the design of electrocatalysts, but the overall system. Sometimes, electrochemistry can be combained with other methods for higher efficiency, such as photoelectrocatalysis.
Our lab focuses on the following systems: 3D printed electrochemical cell and multilayer electrochemical cell.

In Situ Study of Electrochemical Process

Surface detection of electrocatalyst using in situ technique: Raman, AFM, ATR-IR, and UV-vis spectroscopy

Electrochemical interface is where the electron transfer between electrode/electrocatalyst/electrolyte happens. Understanding the interface reveals the structure-property relationship of electrocatalysts. In situ techniques are useful to study the interface.
Our lab focuses on the following techniques: in situ UV-Vis and Raman spectroscopy, Atomic Force Microscopy, and X-ray diffraction.
- **Topic A1**: In situ spectroelectrochemistry for intermediate and electrochemical double layer (EDL) detection
- **Topic A2**: In situ atomic force microscopy coupled scanning electrochemical microscopy (AFM-SECM) for morphologic and structural detection
- **Topic A3**: In situ mass spectroscopy for product detection during electrocatalysis

Rational Material Design for Electrocatalysis

Electrochemical exfoliation and doping of layered materials for electrocatalysis

Electrocatalysts are materials that can promote electrochemical conversion at fix potential. For best efficiency, the materials need to have suitable surface atomic configuration and adorption energy. The best way of achieving this is rational design of active sites.
We study the following reactions: carbon dioxide electrochemical adsorption and reduction, electrochemical organic synthesis, and water splitting reaction (or water electrolysis).
- **Topic B1**: Electrochemical synthesis of inorganic materials, e.g. 2D materials and one-step metal doping 
- **Topic B2**: Single atom supported by polymer for seawater splitting reaction, CO2 reduction reaction, biomass conversion
